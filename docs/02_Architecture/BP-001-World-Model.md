## 1. Purpose

This document defines the World Model of Health One.

The World Model describes how Health One understands the health management ecosystem at the highest structural level.

It does not define detailed product features, database tables, user interfaces, or implementation plans.

It defines:

1. The core participants.
2. The core relationships.
3. The core value flows.
4. The role of 健康元.
5. The role of AI.
6. The role of stores.
7. The role of the platform.
8. The long-term ecosystem logic.

This document should guide future product, architecture, business, AI, and engineering design.

---

## 2. Core Worldview

Health One starts from the following worldview:

> Health is not a one-time transaction.  
> Health is a continuous relationship.  
> A continuous relationship requires memory, trust, service, feedback, and growth.

In the traditional health service world, the customer, store, service provider, product, data, and follow-up process are often disconnected.

Health One aims to reconnect these fragmented parts into a continuously learning health ecosystem.

---

## 3. Fundamental Problem

The fundamental problem Health One addresses is fragmentation.

Fragmentation appears in several forms:

1. Customer health information is fragmented.
2. Store service records are fragmented.
3. AI interactions are disconnected from real services.
4. Products are disconnected from long-term outcomes.
5. Service follow-up is weak or missing.
6. Customer trust is difficult to accumulate.
7. Store operating capability does not compound.
8. Health knowledge is scattered across people, tools, documents, and platforms.

Health One exists to reduce this fragmentation.

---

## 4. Fundamental Design Direction

The fundamental design direction of Health One is:

> Build a continuous health relationship system around the customer's 健康元.

This means Health One should organize:

1. Customer identity.
2. Health memory.
3. AI understanding.
4. Store service.
5. Service records.
6. Follow-up.
7. Knowledge.
8. Trust.
9. Growth.

The system should not be built around isolated transactions.

It should be built around the long-term health relationship.

## 6. Customer

The customer is the central human subject of Health One.

The customer is not merely:

1. A lead.
2. A traffic asset.
3. A transaction record.
4. A member card.
5. A marketing tag.
6. A data point.

The customer is a person with:

1. Health history.
2. Health goals.
3. Concerns.
4. Preferences.
5. Habits.
6. Service experience.
7. Trust relationships.
8. Long-term growth potential.

Health One should be designed around the customer's long-term health journey.

---

## 7. 健康元

健康元 is the customer's personal health identity and health memory inside the Health One ecosystem.

健康元 is not simply a user profile.

It represents:

1. Health identity.
2. Health memory.
3. Health goals.
4. Service history.
5. Follow-up records.
6. AI understanding.
7. Store service context.
8. Long-term health growth trajectory.

健康元 should grow over time.

Every meaningful health interaction may enrich the customer's 健康元.

---

## 8. Customer Ownership Principle

The customer should be treated as the owner of their 健康元.

This means:

1. The customer owns the health identity.
2. The customer owns the long-term health memory.
3. Store access should be based on authorization.
4. Platform analysis should respect permission.
5. AI interaction should operate within authorized context.
6. Health memory should not be treated as merely platform-owned traffic data.

This principle should guide future product, data, AI, and business architecture.

---

## 9. AI Health Companion

The AI Health Companion is the customer-facing AI role in Health One.

Its function is not merely to answer questions.

It should help the customer:

1. Understand health status.
2. Clarify goals.
3. Remember health history.
4. Receive personalized guidance.
5. Prepare for service.
6. Follow up after service.
7. Build long-term awareness.
8. Improve health behavior over time.

The AI Health Companion should be grounded in authorized health memory, project knowledge, store service context, and appropriate health knowledge.

It must not pretend to provide certainty where uncertainty exists.

---

## 10. Store

The store is a real-world health service node.

A store is not merely:

1. A sales outlet.
2. A device location.
3. A booking point.
4. A transaction endpoint.

A Health One store should become:

1. A service station.
2. A follow-up station.
3. A trust node.
4. A customer relationship node.
5. A data and observation node.
6. A health education point.
7. A bridge between AI and real service.

The store is where digital health understanding meets physical service.

---

## 11. Store Staff and Health Manager

Store staff and health managers are human service actors.

They provide:

1. Reception.
2. Communication.
3. Service delivery.
4. Observation.
5. Follow-up.
6. Relationship maintenance.
7. Customer trust building.
8. Feedback collection.

Health One should not design AI as a simple replacement for store staff.

AI should support store staff by improving memory, communication, consistency, and service follow-up.

---

## 12. Device

Devices are service infrastructure inside the Health One world.

At the current business stage, one important device category is the graphene far-infrared health cabin.

A device should not be treated only as a piece of equipment.

In Health One, a device can become:

1. A service delivery point.
2. A usage record source.
3. A customer habit anchor.
4. A health service scenario.
5. A bridge between store operation and customer health memory.

Device usage should contribute to the customer's 健康元 when appropriate.

---

## 13. Service

A service is a structured interaction between customer, store, staff, device, AI, and health memory.

A service should not disappear after the transaction.

A service should generate:

1. Service record.
2. Customer feedback.
3. Store observation.
4. Follow-up trigger.
5. AI context update.
6. Health memory enrichment.
7. Potential next action.

The service is a key moment where Health One converts real-world activity into long-term health memory.

---

## 14. Product

Products may exist inside the Health One ecosystem, but they should not dominate the system logic.

A product should be introduced when it supports:

1. Customer health goals.
2. Store service continuity.
3. Follow-up.
4. Health improvement.
5. Trust.
6. Value loop completion.

Health One should avoid becoming a product-selling system before the health value loop is proven.

---

## 15. Partner

Partners may include:

1. Coaches.
2. Sports venues.
3. Health service providers.
4. Supply chain partners.
5. Professional experts.
6. Business channels.
7. Investment and financing resources.
8. Local operators.

Partners should be integrated when they strengthen the ecosystem.

Partner relationships should not compromise customer trust, data responsibility, or service quality.

## 16. Platform

The platform is the organizing layer of Health One.

The platform should provide:

1. Identity infrastructure.
2. Health memory infrastructure.
3. AI coordination.
4. Store workbench.
5. Knowledge base.
6. Service flow management.
7. Authorization and access control.
8. Data structure.
9. Ecosystem coordination.
10. Long-term learning.

The platform should not treat itself as the only value creator.

Its role is to help customers, stores, staff, partners, and AI create value together.

## 19. Core Relationship: Customer and 健康元

The most important relationship is:

> Customer owns 健康元.  
> 健康元 grows with health memory.  
> Health One serves the growth of 健康元.

This relationship establishes the customer-centered foundation of the system.

The customer is not inside the platform as traffic.

The platform, store, AI, and service should surround the customer's health growth.

---

## 20. Core Relationship: 健康元 and AI

The AI Health Companion should operate through 健康元.

This means:

1. AI reads authorized memory.
2. AI updates understanding through interactions.
3. AI provides guidance based on context.
4. AI supports follow-up.
5. AI helps structure customer health journey.
6. AI explains rather than merely outputs.

Without 健康元, AI becomes a generic chatbot.

With 健康元, AI can become a long-term health companion.

---

## 21. Core Relationship: Store and 健康元

The store serves the customer through authorized access to relevant 健康元 context.

This means:

1. The store can better understand customer needs.
2. Service can become more personalized.
3. Follow-up can become more consistent.
4. Customer trust can accumulate.
5. Store operation can improve over time.

The store should not own the customer's health identity.

The store participates in serving and enriching the customer's health journey.

---

## 22. Core Relationship: Service and Memory

Every meaningful service should generate memory.

This memory may include:

1. What service was performed.
2. When it happened.
3. Why it was selected.
4. Customer condition before service.
5. Customer feedback after service.
6. Staff observation.
7. AI follow-up result.
8. Suggested next step.

Without memory, service remains isolated.

With memory, service becomes part of a long-term health journey.

---

## 23. Core Relationship: Platform and Ecosystem

The platform organizes the ecosystem.

It should coordinate:

1. Customer identity.
2. Store service.
3. AI interaction.
4. Device usage.
5. Product and service knowledge.
6. Partner participation.
7. Data and authorization.
8. Long-term learning.

The platform should not extract value without strengthening the ecosystem.

The platform should become more valuable as the ecosystem becomes healthier.

---

## 24. Value Flow

The primary value flow of Health One is:

1. Customer expresses a health need.
2. AI helps understand and structure the need.
3. Store or service provider delivers real service.
4. Service generates record and feedback.
5. 健康元 grows.
6. AI becomes more useful.
7. Store understands the customer better.
8. Customer receives better follow-up.
9. Trust increases.
10. The customer returns.
11. The ecosystem learns.

This is the basic compounding loop.

---

## 25. Data Flow

The basic data flow is:

1. Customer input.
2. AI interaction.
3. Health record.
4. Store service record.
5. Device usage record.
6. Customer feedback.
7. Staff observation.
8. Follow-up result.
9. AI insight.
10. Updated 健康元.

Data should serve health understanding, service continuity, trust, and customer value.

Data should not be collected without purpose.

---

## 26. Trust Flow

Trust is built through repeated reliable interactions.

Trust flow includes:

1. Clear explanation.
2. Authorized data use.
3. Responsible AI response.
4. Real service delivery.
5. Consistent follow-up.
6. Transparent service record.
7. Respect for customer ownership.
8. Improvement over time.

Trust should be designed as a system property, not left to chance.

---

## 27. Learning Flow

Health One should continuously learn from:

1. Customer interactions.
2. Service outcomes.
3. Store operations.
4. AI conversations.
5. Staff feedback.
6. Customer retention.
7. Partner cooperation.
8. Research and market changes.
9. Product experiments.
10. Engineering implementation.

Learning should be structured and fed back into the Knowledge Base, AI design, product design, and store operations.

---

## 28. Ecosystem Growth Model

Health One should grow through ecosystem compounding.

The growth logic is:

1. Better memory improves AI.
2. Better AI improves customer guidance.
3. Better guidance improves service matching.
4. Better service improves trust.
5. Better trust improves retention.
6. Better retention improves data and feedback.
7. Better data improves store operation.
8. Better store operation improves ecosystem value.
9. Better ecosystem value attracts partners.
10. Better partners expand service capability.

This is the long-term growth engine.

---

## 29. What the World Model Rejects

This World Model rejects the following assumptions:

1. Customers are merely traffic.
2. Stores are merely sales points.
3. Devices are merely machines.
4. AI is merely a chatbot.
5. Data is merely platform property.
6. Products should dominate before service value is proven.
7. Automation should replace trust.
8. Growth should come before service consistency.
9. Engineering should outrun product clarity.
10. Chat history can replace formal knowledge base.
