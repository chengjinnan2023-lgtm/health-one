## 1. Purpose

This document defines the lifecycle of 健康元 inside Health One.

BP-001 defines the World Model.  
BP-002 defines the MVP and first value loop.  
BP-003 defines how a customer's 健康元 is created, activated, served, enriched, followed up, and grown over time.

The purpose of this document is to ensure that Health One is designed around a long-term customer health journey rather than isolated transactions.

---

## 2. Lifecycle Principle

The core lifecycle principle is:

> 健康元 is not created once and then stored.  
> 健康元 is continuously activated, enriched, interpreted, and grown through real health interactions.

A customer's 健康元 should become more useful over time.

If Health One does not make 健康元 grow, it becomes only a profile or account.

If 健康元 grows through service, memory, AI, store interaction, and follow-up, it becomes the core health identity of the customer.

---

## 3. Lifecycle Stages

The Health One lifecycle includes the following stages:

1. Awareness.
2. Entry.
3. Activation.
4. Initial Understanding.
5. Service Matching.
6. Store Service.
7. Service Recording.
8. Feedback.
9. AI Follow-Up.
10. Memory Enrichment.
11. Return.
12. Growth.
13. Re-Engagement.
14. Long-Term Health Relationship.

These stages may repeat and deepen over time.

---

## 4. Stage 1: Awareness

Awareness is the stage where a potential customer first encounters Health One.

Possible awareness sources include:

1. Store visit.
2. Coach recommendation.
3. Sports venue cooperation.
4. Health content.
5. Social media.
6. Existing customer referral.
7. Offline activity.
8. Partner channel.

The goal of this stage is not immediate conversion only.

The goal is to introduce the idea that Health One can support continuous health management.

---

## 5. Stage 2: Entry

Entry is the stage where the customer enters the Health One system.

Possible entry points include:

1. Store staff creates the customer record.
2. Customer scans a code.
3. Customer joins a mini-program or app.
4. Customer receives an invitation link.
5. Customer is introduced by a coach or partner.
6. Customer participates in a store activity.

The entry experience should be simple.

The customer should understand:

1. What 健康元 is.
2. Why it helps.
3. What information may be recorded.
4. How it supports follow-up.
5. How the store and AI will use it.

---

## 6. Stage 3: Activation

Activation means the customer's 健康元 becomes usable.

Activation may include:

1. Basic identity creation.
2. Main health concern recording.
3. Health goal setting.
4. Store relationship binding.
5. Consent or authorization.
6. First AI summary.
7. First service intention.

Activation should not require excessive data collection.

The early goal is to create enough context to support the first value loop.

---

## 7. Stage 4: Initial Understanding

Initial Understanding is the stage where Health One begins to understand the customer.

This understanding may come from:

1. Customer self-description.
2. Store staff observation.
3. Health concern intake.
4. AI conversation.
5. Previous service history.
6. Device usage.
7. Uploaded materials when available.
8. Customer goals.

The output should be a simple initial health context, not an overconfident diagnosis.

The system should distinguish between:

1. Known facts.
2. Customer self-reported information.
3. Staff observations.
4. AI interpretations.
5. Uncertain areas.

---

## 8. Stage 5: Service Matching

Service Matching connects customer needs with appropriate store services.

The system may help match:

1. Customer concern.
2. Store service.
3. Device usage.
4. Staff recommendation.
5. Follow-up plan.
6. Health goal.

At the MVP stage, service matching should remain simple and explainable.

The goal is not to automate all decisions.

The goal is to help staff and customers make more consistent, context-aware service decisions.

---

## 9. Stage 6: Store Service

Store Service is the real-world service stage.

It may include:

1. Reception.
2. Communication.
3. Service explanation.
4. Device usage.
5. Health management service.
6. Staff observation.
7. Customer experience.
8. Service completion.

The store is where Health One becomes real.

A digital system without service execution cannot prove the Health One value loop.

---

## 10. Stage 7: Service Recording

Service Recording turns real-world service into structured memory.

A service record should capture the minimum useful data needed for future understanding.

Recommended record elements include:

1. Customer.
2. Store.
3. Staff.
4. Service type.
5. Service time.
6. Customer concern.
7. Device usage if applicable.
8. Staff observation.
9. Customer feedback.
10. Follow-up suggestion.
11. AI summary.

The record should be easy for staff to complete.

If recording is too difficult, staff will not use it and the lifecycle will break.

---

## 11. Stage 8: Feedback

Feedback captures the customer's response after service.

Feedback may include:

1. Immediate feeling.
2. Comfort level.
3. Pain or fatigue change.
4. Satisfaction.
5. Questions.
6. Willingness to return.
7. Concern about next steps.

Feedback does not need to be complex at the beginning.

The MVP should capture simple, useful feedback that can support follow-up and memory growth.

---

## 12. Stage 9: AI Follow-Up

AI Follow-Up extends the service relationship after the customer leaves the store.

AI follow-up may include:

1. Service summary.
2. Customer feedback request.
3. Reminder.
4. Health guidance.
5. Next service suggestion.
6. Progress check.
7. Question handling.
8. Staff escalation when necessary.

Follow-up should be responsible and bounded.

AI should not provide unsupported medical certainty.

AI should focus on continuity, explanation, reminders, and customer support.

---

## 13. Stage 10: Memory Enrichment

Memory Enrichment is the stage where the customer's 健康元 becomes more complete.

The system may enrich memory through:

1. Service records.
2. Feedback.
3. AI conversation.
4. Staff observation.
5. Device usage.
6. Customer goals.
7. Return visits.
8. Changes over time.

Memory enrichment should make future service better.

If memory is recorded but not used, it does not create value.

---

## 14. Stage 11: Return

Return means the customer comes back into the loop.

Return may happen through:

1. Follow-up recommendation.
2. Store invitation.
3. AI check-in.
4. Customer self-initiated booking.
5. Coach recommendation.
6. Health goal continuation.
7. Service package usage.
8. Progress review.

Return is a key validation signal.

A Health One lifecycle is working when customers have a clear reason to continue.

---

## 15. Stage 12: Growth

Growth means the customer's health journey becomes more structured over time.

Growth may appear as:

1. More complete health memory.
2. Clearer goals.
3. Better service matching.
4. Improved follow-up.
5. Stronger trust.
6. Better customer understanding.
7. More repeat service.
8. More useful AI assistance.
9. Better store relationship.

Growth is not only customer growth.

The store, AI, platform, and knowledge base should also grow through the loop.

---

## 16. Stage 13: Re-Engagement

Re-Engagement is needed when the customer becomes inactive.

Possible re-engagement methods include:

1. AI check-in.
2. Store staff follow-up.
3. Health reminder.
4. Progress summary.
5. Service recommendation.
6. Activity invitation.
7. Coach or partner reminder.

Re-engagement should be respectful.

It should not become aggressive marketing.

The purpose is to reconnect the customer with health value, not merely push consumption.

---

## 17. Stage 14: Long-Term Health Relationship

The long-term goal is to establish an ongoing health relationship.

In this stage, Health One should support:

1. Long-term memory.
2. Continuous follow-up.
3. Periodic health review.
4. Store relationship maintenance.
5. AI companion continuity.
6. Service plan evolution.
7. Partner service integration.
8. Customer trust accumulation.

At this stage, 健康元 becomes a long-term health identity rather than a short-term service record.

---

## 18. Lifecycle Loop

The lifecycle is not linear only.

It should repeat and deepen:

```text
Awareness
→ Entry
→ Activation
→ Initial Understanding
→ Service Matching
→ Store Service
→ Service Recording
→ Feedback
→ AI Follow-Up
→ Memory Enrichment
→ Return
→ Growth
→ Re-Engagement
→ Long-Term Health Relationship
→ New Service Loop
```

Each repeated loop should make the next loop better.

---

## 19. Lifecycle Data

The lifecycle may generate the following data:

1. Identity data.
2. Health concern data.
3. Goal data.
4. Service data.
5. Device usage data.
6. Staff observation data.
7. Customer feedback data.
8. AI interaction data.
9. Follow-up data.
10. Return behavior data.
11. Growth summary data.
12. Authorization data.

Data should be collected only when it supports service, memory, trust, or learning.

---

## 20. Lifecycle and Customer Ownership

The customer should remain the owner of their 健康元 throughout the lifecycle.

This means:

1. The customer should understand the purpose of 健康元.
2. Data use should be explainable.
3. Store access should be authorized.
4. AI use should be context-bounded.
5. The platform should protect health memory responsibly.
6. Customer trust should guide lifecycle design.

The lifecycle must not turn health memory into hidden platform property.

---

## 21. Lifecycle and Store Operation

The lifecycle should help stores improve operation.

A store should benefit through:

1. Better customer understanding.
2. Easier service recording.
3. More consistent follow-up.
4. Better repeat service.
5. Improved staff communication.
6. Stronger customer relationship.
7. Service quality improvement.
8. More structured operating data.

If the lifecycle increases store burden without improving service, it needs redesign.

---

## 22. Lifecycle and AI

AI supports the lifecycle by:

1. Structuring intake.
2. Summarizing context.
3. Explaining services.
4. Supporting follow-up.
5. Remembering customer changes.
6. Helping staff communicate.
7. Retrieving knowledge.
8. Detecting missing information.
9. Generating next-step suggestions.
10. Maintaining continuity.

AI should serve the lifecycle.

AI should not become an isolated chatbot outside the service loop.

---

## 23. Lifecycle and Knowledge Base

The lifecycle should feed the knowledge base.

Lessons may come from:

1. Common customer concerns.
2. Effective follow-up patterns.
3. Store service observations.
4. AI interaction issues.
5. Customer return behavior.
6. Service explanation improvements.
7. Staff operating feedback.
8. Partner scenario experience.

The knowledge base should then improve AI, product, store operation, and training.

---

## 24. Lifecycle Success Signals

The lifecycle is working if:

1. Customers understand 健康元.
2. Customers are willing to activate 健康元.
3. Staff can record services easily.
4. AI follow-up is useful.
5. Service memory improves next interaction.
6. Customers return.
7. Stores see operating value.
8. Customer trust increases.
9. The knowledge base accumulates useful learning.
10. The loop repeats with more customers.

---

## 25. Lifecycle Failure Signals

The lifecycle may be failing if:

1. Customers do not understand why 健康元 matters.
2. Activation is too complicated.
3. Staff do not record services.
4. AI follow-up is generic.
5. Memory is recorded but not used.
6. Customers do not return.
7. Store staff feel burdened.
8. Trust decreases.
9. Data collection feels excessive.
10. The lifecycle becomes a one-time transaction.

These signals should trigger review.

---

## 26. MVP Lifecycle Scope

At the MVP stage, the lifecycle should remain simple.

The MVP lifecycle should include:

1. Entry.
2. Activation.
3. Initial understanding.
4. Store service.
5. Service record.
6. Feedback.
7. AI follow-up.
8. Memory enrichment.
9. Return.

The MVP does not need to implement the full long-term lifecycle immediately.

But it should be designed so the long-term lifecycle can grow naturally.

---

## 27. Lifecycle Boundary

This document does not define:

1. Database schema.
2. User interface details.
3. Legal consent text.
4. Full medical decision logic.
5. Full AI prompt design.
6. Full store SOP.
7. Complete CRM process.

These should be handled in later product, architecture, engineering, legal, or business documents.

---

## 28. Current Baseline Lifecycle Decisions

As of this document version, the following lifecycle decisions are Baseline:

1. 健康元 should grow over time.
2. The lifecycle begins before the first service and continues after service.
3. Store service must generate memory.
4. Follow-up is part of the lifecycle, not an optional add-on.
5. AI should support lifecycle continuity.
6. Staff usability is critical.
7. Customer ownership applies throughout the lifecycle.
8. Return behavior is a key validation signal.
9. The MVP should implement a simplified but complete lifecycle.
10. Long-term lifecycle design should avoid aggressive marketing and prioritize trust.

---

## 29. End of Document

BP-003 defines the lifecycle of 健康元 inside Health One.

Future product, AI, store operation, and engineering design should support this lifecycle.
