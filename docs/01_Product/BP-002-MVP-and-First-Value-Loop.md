## 1. Purpose

This document defines the MVP direction and first value loop of Health One.

The purpose of this document is to prevent early product development from becoming a scattered feature list.

Health One should not begin by building every possible module.

It should begin by proving one complete value loop.

This document defines:

1. What the MVP means in Health One.
2. What the first value loop is.
3. What the MVP includes.
4. What the MVP excludes.
5. What should be validated before expansion.
6. How product, business, AI, and engineering should align around the first loop.

---

## 2. MVP Principle

In Health One, MVP does not mean the smallest number of features.

MVP means:

> The shortest complete value loop that can prove Health One creates repeatable health value.

A feature is not part of the MVP simply because it is useful.

A feature belongs in the MVP only if it directly strengthens the first complete value loop.

---

## 3. First Value Loop

The first value loop of Health One is:

1. Customer creates or activates a Health One identity.
2. AI builds initial health understanding.
3. AI provides personalized guidance.
4. Customer books or receives store service.
5. Store completes the service.
6. Service result is recorded.
7. AI follows up.
8. Health memory grows.
9. Customer returns.
10. The loop repeats.

This is the first loop Health One must prove.

---

## 4. Why the First Loop Matters

The first loop matters because Health One is not only trying to complete a transaction.

It is trying to prove that:

1. A customer can build a health identity.
2. AI can help create useful health understanding.
3. Store service can be connected to digital memory.
4. Service records can improve future guidance.
5. Follow-up can increase trust.
6. Health memory can grow over time.
7. The customer has a reason to return.
8. The store gains operating capability.
9. The platform becomes more useful through real service.
10. The ecosystem can compound.

If this loop is not proven, expanding into larger systems is premature.

---

## 5. MVP Core User

The first MVP should focus on one primary customer type:

> A customer who has a real health concern, receives store service, and can benefit from follow-up and long-term memory.

The early MVP should not attempt to serve every possible user type.

It should prioritize users who can experience the complete loop.

Possible early customer scenarios include:

1. Shoulder, neck, waist, or back discomfort.
2. Fatigue after exercise.
3. Recovery after sports activity.
4. Weight management demand.
5. Sub-health condition management.
6. Repeated store service users.
7. Customers willing to receive AI follow-up.

---

## 6. MVP Core Store Scenario

The first MVP should focus on real store service scenarios.

The early store scenario includes:

1. Customer arrives at store.
2. Store staff receives the customer.
3. Customer need is recorded.
4. Service is recommended or delivered.
5. Device or service usage is recorded.
6. Customer feedback is captured.
7. AI or store staff follows up.
8. Customer is encouraged to return when appropriate.

The store is not just a transaction location.

The store is the real-world execution node of the value loop.

---

## 7. MVP Core Service

The first MVP should connect at least one real service to the Health One loop.

At the current stage, the graphene far-infrared health cabin may serve as one important service infrastructure.

The service should be treated not merely as equipment usage, but as a structured health interaction.

A service should generate:

1. Service date.
2. Service type.
3. Customer need.
4. Staff observation.
5. Device usage record.
6. Customer feedback.
7. Follow-up trigger.
8. AI memory update.
9. Suggested next action.

---

## 8. MVP Core Components

The MVP should include the following core components:

1. Health One identity.
2. 健康元 profile.
3. AI health companion.
4. Store workbench.
5. Service record.
6. Follow-up mechanism.
7. Basic knowledge base.
8. Basic authorization logic.
9. Basic customer return pathway.

These are the minimum components required to complete the first value loop.

---

## 9. Health One Identity

The Health One identity is the customer's entry point into the ecosystem.

It should allow the system to recognize the customer over time.

The identity should support:

1. Basic customer profile.
2. Health goals.
3. Health concerns.
4. Service history.
5. AI interaction history.
6. Store relationship context.
7. Follow-up state.

The identity should be designed as a long-term health identity, not only as a login account.

---

## 10. 健康元 Profile

The 健康元 profile is the structured representation of the customer's health memory.

It may include:

1. Basic health information.
2. Health goals.
3. Main concerns.
4. Service records.
5. Device usage records.
6. AI summaries.
7. Store observations.
8. Follow-up results.
9. Customer feedback.
10. Health journey timeline.

The early profile should be simple but extensible.

It should not attempt to capture all possible health data at the beginning.

---

## 11. AI Health Companion

The AI Health Companion should support the first loop by helping the customer:

1. Explain health concerns.
2. Clarify goals.
3. Understand service relevance.
4. Prepare for store service.
5. Receive post-service follow-up.
6. Remember changes over time.
7. Build trust through continuity.

The early AI companion should not attempt to replace doctors or professional diagnosis.

It should focus on guidance, memory, explanation, and follow-up within appropriate boundaries.

---

## 12. Store Workbench

The Store Workbench is the store-side operating tool.

It should help staff:

1. Identify customer.
2. Understand customer context.
3. Record service.
4. Record observations.
5. Trigger follow-up.
6. Review customer history.
7. Support repeat service.
8. Improve customer relationship management.

The Store Workbench should make the store more capable, not more burdened.

---

## 13. Service Record

The Service Record is a core object of the MVP.

Each service record should capture the minimum information needed to support the loop.

Recommended early fields include:

1. Customer.
2. Store.
3. Service type.
4. Date and time.
5. Customer concern.
6. Service delivered.
7. Device usage if applicable.
8. Staff observation.
9. Customer feedback.
10. Follow-up action.
11. AI summary.

The Service Record connects real service to long-term memory.

---

## 14. Follow-Up Mechanism

Follow-up is essential to Health One.

Without follow-up, the service remains isolated.

The MVP should include a simple follow-up mechanism.

It may include:

1. AI follow-up message.
2. Store staff follow-up task.
3. Customer feedback collection.
4. Reminder for next service.
5. Summary update to 健康元.
6. Escalation to staff when needed.

The follow-up mechanism should prove that Health One can maintain continuity after the customer leaves the store.

---

## 15. Basic Knowledge Base

The MVP should include a basic knowledge base.

This knowledge base may include:

1. Store service explanations.
2. Device explanations.
3. Common customer concern guidance.
4. Follow-up scripts.
5. Service precautions.
6. Product or service knowledge.
7. Internal operating guidance.

The knowledge base should support AI responses and store staff consistency.

It should not become too complex before the MVP loop is proven.

---

## 16. Authorization Logic

Even in the MVP, Health One should respect authorization.

Basic authorization logic should distinguish:

1. What the customer can see.
2. What the store can see.
3. What staff can record.
4. What AI can use.
5. What the platform can analyze.
6. What requires customer permission.

The MVP does not need a complex legal system, but it should not ignore the customer ownership principle.

---

## 17. Customer Return Pathway

The first loop is only complete if the customer has a reason to return.

The MVP should include at least one customer return pathway, such as:

1. Follow-up recommendation.
2. Next service reminder.
3. Health progress summary.
4. Store invitation.
5. AI check-in.
6. Service package recommendation.
7. Customer goal tracking.

Return behavior is one of the most important signs that the loop is working.

---

## 18. MVP Exclusions

The MVP should not prioritize:

1. Full marketplace.
2. Complex franchise system.
3. Token mechanism.
4. Blockchain.
5. Full contribution monetization.
6. Full digital human system.
7. Overbuilt CRM.
8. Overbuilt ERP.
9. Complex membership hierarchy.
10. Large-scale automated marketing.
11. Multi-region operating system.
12. Advanced financial settlement system.
13. Full partner ecosystem management.

These may be future modules, but they should not distract the first value loop.

---

## 19. MVP Success Criteria

The MVP should be judged by whether the first value loop works.

Possible success criteria include:

1. Customers can create or activate 健康元.
2. Store staff can record real service.
3. AI can generate useful follow-up.
4. Customers understand the value of long-term memory.
5. Customers are willing to return.
6. Store staff feel the system helps service.
7. Follow-up improves customer relationship.
8. Service records enrich future interaction.
9. The store can see operational value.
10. The loop can repeat with multiple customers.

The MVP should not be judged only by feature completeness.

---

## 20. MVP Non-Success Signals

The MVP may be failing if:

1. Customers do not understand why they need 健康元.
2. Store staff do not use the workbench.
3. Service records are not created.
4. Follow-up does not happen.
5. AI responses feel generic.
6. Customers do not return.
7. The system increases staff burden without improving service.
8. Data is collected but not used.
9. The loop ends after one transaction.
10. The store sees no operating improvement.

These signals should be monitored early.

---

## 21. Product Priority Rule

Product priorities should be evaluated by one question:

> Does this feature strengthen the first value loop?

If yes, it may be considered.

If no, it should be delayed.

This rule protects the MVP from becoming too large too early.

---

## 22. AI Priority Rule

AI priorities should focus on:

1. Health memory.
2. Customer understanding.
3. Personalized explanation.
4. Follow-up.
5. Store staff assistance.
6. Knowledge base retrieval.
7. Trust-building.
8. Context continuity.

AI should not be used first for superficial automation if it does not improve the value loop.

---

## 23. Store Priority Rule

Store-side priorities should focus on:

1. Reducing staff burden.
2. Improving customer understanding.
3. Making service records easy.
4. Supporting follow-up.
5. Increasing repeat visits.
6. Improving trust.
7. Helping staff communicate better.

The store workbench must be practical.

If store staff do not use it, the MVP loop breaks.

---

## 24. Engineering Priority Rule

Engineering should prioritize:

1. Identity.
2. Service record.
3. Health memory.
4. AI context.
5. Store workbench.
6. Follow-up.
7. Basic knowledge base.
8. Authorization.
9. Simple analytics.

Engineering should not prioritize complex infrastructure before the loop is proven.

---

## 25. Business Priority Rule

Business activities should focus on scenarios where the first loop can be tested.

Priority business scenarios include:

1. Existing stores.
2. Existing device usage.
3. Real customer service.
4. Coach or sports venue cooperation when it brings customers into the loop.
5. Health management service packages.
6. Repeat service users.
7. Staff who can cooperate with follow-up.

Business development should avoid expanding faster than service consistency.

---

## 26. First MVP Operating Scenario

A simple operating scenario:

1. A customer enters the store with fatigue or shoulder and neck discomfort.
2. Staff creates or activates the customer's 健康元.
3. AI or staff records the customer's main concern.
4. Staff recommends an appropriate service.
5. Customer uses the graphene far-infrared health cabin or receives another approved service.
6. Staff records the service.
7. Customer gives simple feedback.
8. AI generates a follow-up summary.
9. Customer receives a follow-up message.
10. Customer returns or continues the health journey.

This scenario is sufficient to test the first loop.

---

## 27. Data Needed for First Loop

The first loop needs only the minimum useful data.

Recommended early data includes:

1. Customer name or identifier.
2. Contact method.
3. Main health concern.
4. Health goal.
5. Store.
6. Service date.
7. Service type.
8. Staff observation.
9. Customer feedback.
10. Follow-up status.
11. AI summary.
12. Next suggested action.

More data can be added later after actual usage proves necessity.

---

## 28. Avoiding False MVP

A false MVP may appear functional but fail to prove the loop.

Examples of false MVP:

1. A beautiful interface with no service follow-up.
2. A chatbot with no memory.
3. A booking system with no health understanding.
4. A customer database with no return pathway.
5. A device usage log with no follow-up.
6. A marketplace with no trust relationship.
7. A CRM with no customer-owned health identity.
8. A content account with no connection to service.

Health One must avoid building a false MVP.

---

## 29. MVP and Trust

Trust must be present from the beginning.

The MVP should communicate clearly:

1. What information is recorded.
2. Why it is recorded.
3. How it helps the customer.
4. Who can see it.
5. How follow-up works.
6. What AI can and cannot do.

Trust should not be postponed to a later stage.

---

## 30. MVP and Knowledge Base

The MVP should connect to the knowledge base from the beginning.

The knowledge base should help:

1. AI provide consistent answers.
2. Staff explain services.
3. Follow-up become more reliable.
4. Service recommendations become more structured.
5. Project learning accumulate.

The knowledge base is not separate from MVP.

It is part of the MVP foundation.

---

## 31. MVP and Future Expansion

If the first value loop works, Health One can gradually expand into:

1. More services.
2. More stores.
3. More devices.
4. More customer scenarios.
5. More AI capabilities.
6. More partner models.
7. More business models.
8. More advanced analytics.
9. More ecosystem mechanisms.

Expansion should follow proof of the loop.

---

## 32. Current Baseline MVP Decisions

As of this document version, the following MVP decisions are Baseline:

1. MVP means the shortest complete value loop, not the smallest feature set.
2. The first value loop starts with customer identity and ends with repeat use.
3. 健康元, AI health companion, store workbench, service record, follow-up, and knowledge base are core MVP components.
4. The MVP should be tested through real store service scenarios.
5. Store staff usability is critical.
6. Follow-up is not optional.
7. Health memory must grow through real service.
8. Customer return behavior is a key validation signal.
9. Premature complexity should be avoided.
10. Engineering should support the loop, not outrun it.

---

## 33. End of Document

BP-002 defines the MVP and first value loop of Health One.

Future product, architecture, AI, engineering, and business work should remain aligned with this value loop.
